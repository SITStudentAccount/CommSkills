Introduction Facial Expression

Facial expression is integral when expressing emotions through the body. Combinations of eyes, eyebrow, lips, nose, and cheek movements help form different moods of an individual (e.g. happy, sad, depressed, angry, etc.). It is vital to social communication between humans.

Effects of Reading Facial Expression

Recognizing how other people feel serves as a major social asset. Whether you accurately interpret a quick glance from a partner, or you notice your neighbour's disinterest in hearing your story, perceiving other people’s emotions correctly improves communication.

Learning to Read Faces and Facial Expression

1. Facial expressions of certain emotions were universal.

2. Facial expressions reliably associated certain emotional states.

7 Major Types of Facial Expression

A facial expression is motions and positions of the muscles on the face.These motions and positions can be translated to the emotional state of an individual. Facial expressions are a form of nonverbal communication and are a primary means of conveying social information.

  • Happiness

    Mental or emotional state of well-being defined by positive or pleasant emotions

  • Sadness

    Emotional pain associated with feelings of loss, despair, grief, helplessness, disappointment and sorrow.

  • Contempt

    Feeling of disliking and having no respect for someone or something

  • Disgust

    Feeling of revulsion or strong disapproval aroused by something unpleasant or offensive

  • Surprise

    Feeling caused by something unexpected happening

  • Fear

    Unpleasant mental or emotional state caused by the threat of danger, pain, or harm

  • Anger

    Intense mental or emotional state involves a strong uncomfortable and emotional response to a perceived provocation, hurt or threat

Learning to Read Happiness

1. A face expressing happiness or joy will feature a smile (corners of the mouth drawn up and back) with some teeth exposed, and a wrinkle runs from the outer nose to the outer corners of the lip.

2. The cheeks are raised, and the lower eyelids are tense or wrinkled.

3. The narrowing of the eyelids causes “crow’s feet” wrinkles at the outer corners of the eyes.

Learning to Read Sadness

1. Face showing sadness has the eyebrows drawn in and up.

2. The skin below the eyebrows is triangulated with the inner corner up.

3. The corners of the lips are turned down.

4. The jaw comes up and the bottom lip pouts out.

Learning to Read Disgust

1. A disgusted face has the eyebrows downcast, but

2. The lower eyelid raised (causing the eyes to narrow)

3. The cheeks are raised and the nose is scrunched.

4. The upper lip is also raised or curled upward.

Learning to Read Surprise

1. The skin below the brow is stretched and there are horizontal wrinkles across the forehead.

2. A surprised face features the eyebrows raised up and curved.

3. The eyelids are so wide open that the whites are showing above and/or below the pupils.

4. there’s no stretching or tension of the mouth.

5. The jaw is dropped and teeth are slightly parted.

Learning to Read Fear

1. There are wrinkles in the forehead in the center between the brows, not across.

2. A face showing fear has raised eyebrows that are usually more flat, not curved.

3. The upper eyelids are raised.

4. Lower eyelids are tense and drawn up, usually causing whites to show in the upper eye but not lower.

5. Nostrils may be flared.

6. The lips are usually tensed or drawn back, the mouth may be open.

Learning to Read Sadness

1. An angry face will show eyebrows that are lowered and drawn together.

2. Eyes staring hard or bulging, with vertical lines appearing between the brows and the lower eyelids tensed.

3. Nostrils may be flared, and

4. Mouth is either firmly pressed together with the lips drawn down at the corners, or in a square shape as if shouting.

5. Lower jaw juts out.

Learning to MircoExpression

A microexpression is a shortened version of an emotional facial expression. They go on and off the face in a fraction of a second, sometimes 1/30 of a second. They happen so fast that if you blink you can miss them.

Microexpressions are usually the sign of concealed emotions. Sometimes the emotions are not necessarily being concealed, they are just rapidly processed.

Research suggests that micro expressions happen because facial expressions cannot be completely voluntarily controlled, even if the person is trying to control their emotions. There are two neural pathways in the brain that mediates facial expressions, and they enter into a type of “tug of war” over the face when someone is in an intensely emotional situation but is trying to conceal their feelings

A microexpression is a brief, involuntary facial expression that is shown on the face of humans according to the emotions that are being experienced. Unlike regular pro-longed facial expressions, it is difficult to fake a microexpression.
